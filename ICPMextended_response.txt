Here is a summary of the changes made to the extended journal version of the Timed Alignments paper.

1. The abstract, the introduction, the conclusion, and the title of section 7 have been edited to better reflect the actual contributions and focus of our paper.

2. The introduction now clearly states that this is an extension of the existing conference version, and the changes in this paper have been highlighted.

3. The introduction also clearly argues why the classes of models we have solved the purely timed alignment for (namely bounded and extended free choice TPNs) are practical, and not too structurally restricted. Boundedness is essential for most properties about TPNs to be decidable at all, and extended free choice only excludes TPNs that exhibit a behaviour known as confusion (see Aura and Lilius' article [18] on Time Processes for Time Petri Nets) which is not considered a desirable property for process models to begin with, as it lets causally unrelated transitions affect each others' firing times.

4. The preliminaries have been edited to be easier to grasp, and typographical errors have been addressed.

5. The implementation section has been removed.

6. A related work section has been added, which details several other approaches to distances for timed words, and alignments over time-series.

7. Language has been adjusted throughout to address most of the ambiguities mentioned. Some comments did not seem to apply to the draft that was submitted (we could find no instance when \lambda was used to denote a timing function, for instance)

8. Improved the running examples to provide more context for the design choices, and better readability. Examples have been made larger, repeated closer to each instance of usage, and given clear captions.

9. It has been suggested that we rely on examples too much for design choices. We have three types of examples in the paper, the first being simply an illustration of a formal definition or an execution of an algorithm we defined, in order to help the reader's conceptual understanding, including examples 1,2,3,6,7,10 and 11. The second type is used to provide witnesses to behaviours that show a limitation of a particular approach, and hence suggest that a different solution might be necessary at times (hence, these types of examples need not generalise to all scenarios), including examples 4 and 12. The third type of example, example 5, seeks to provide intuition as to why we chose to consider the edit moves we did. This was a design choice that was backed up by its clear conceptual meaning and relevance to practical settings. Beyond a point, this choice is slightly arbitrary, but as evidenced by the fact that in the article Robust Timed Automata by Gupta, Henzinger and Jagadeesan, the first two metrics used also similarly stamp and delay based, these edit moves are some of the simplest and most practical ones to consider for timed sequences.

10. We also take care to point out here that our method for bounded TPNs for aligning under $d_t$ encodes it as a linear programming problem, not an integer linear programming problem. The latter has a much higher complexity (NP rather than P) and is hence much less efficient than our approach.

> Reviewer 1: The paper describes an interesting problem: How to detect 
> issues with timing in event logs and models with time information. 
> Traditionally in process mining, time is ignored for the alignment

> the timestamp outside of the causal ordering of the trace imposed by the 
> model) and the delay move, which ensures that the change of timestamp

> 
> The complexity of the techniques presented is clearly explained, with it 
> being exponential for the general case (ILP) and quadratic for the 
> sequential model case.

> there are very tiny and unlabeled. The figure in Example 3 is wrong

> Since I was reviewer for the conference as well, I report here my

> The paper addresses the complex topic of timed alignments, i.e., 
> proposes an approach for aligning a timed trace (including pairs of 
> activities and timestamps) and a Labeled Time Petri net.
> 
> The paper is well written. I appreciated the fact that the authors 
> provide several examples that allow the reader to understand the 
> different notions provided in the paper.
> 
> However, considering also the fact that the problem has a very high 
> complexity, the solution is rather limited. The authors had to put 
> strong assumptions to solve the problem efficiently and I am not totally 
> convinced that the solution provided is of practical utility. First of 
> all, a first assumption is that the trace to be aligned is compliant 
> with the Petri net in terms of control flow. Starting from this 
> assumption the alignment problem can be solved by using Linear 
> Programming. However, as one can expect, when the Petri net contains 
> parallel branches, the problem becomes difficult to deal with. 
> Therefore, the authors are forced to add an additional (strong) 
> assumption, i.e., the assumption that the model is sequential.
> 
> Although I am not totally convinced about the practical usage of the 
> proposed solution under these assumptions (the authors should put a 
> little bit more effort in motivate the usefulness of their solution), in 
> my opinion, the contribution could still be valid if the focus of the 
> paper is in the definition of the problem. To this aim, I think that the 
> authors can merge the problem presented in this paper with the 
> properties provided in paper 29 that I have also reviewed.
> 
> The combination of the two papers could provide a sufficient 
> contribution in terms of characterization of the problem and of the 
> desiderata of the solution.
> 
> An additional issue I have found in this paper is also that the 
> evaluation is extremely poor. It consist of a table reporting some 
> execution times but the experimental setting is not clear and, in the 
> way it is presented, it does not say much about the scalability of the 
> approach. If the paper becomes focused on the theoretical definition of 
> the problem, this issue becomes less significant, although it is still 
> required that the authors provide more details about the experimental 
> setting.

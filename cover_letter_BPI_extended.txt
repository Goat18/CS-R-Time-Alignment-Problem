Here is a summary of changes made. Then we give precise responses to the comments given.

1. Time Petri net process models have some implicit assumptions about the independence of the resources employed for different events, but of course more sophisticated models exist that explicitly express data and resource constraints. This is a very interesting consideration, and has been duly mentioned in section 6 now as an important possible future extension, but the matter is outside the scope of this paper.

2. Example 1 has been corrected, and the argument should work well now.

3. Definition 5 is correct. We emphasise in the paper now that in our definition we disallow costs being zero, as if either cost was zero the distance would be trivially zero for all pairs of words. d_0, the stamp only distance, is only achievable if the stamp cost is a finite number, and the delay cost is infinite, and hence any minimal cost sequence of moves will be delay only.

4. The use of mixed move notation on page 5 has been replaced with an English description.

5. The typo in Lemma 1 has been fixed, the definition is now correct.

6. Sections 3, 4 and 5 have been edited, and more explanation has been added especially for algorithms and the theorems in section 3.5 and 4. More motivation is provided, and the overall structure of the argument should be much clearer now.

7. The implementation section has received more explanation and context. We consider the main result of this paper to be the theoretical results on runtime complexity, and the results presented in Section 5 are simply an empirical confirmation of the same. Comments from the BPI 2023 workshop also discussed extending the scope of this implementation, but the algorithms presented and the implementation here are very straightforward. As such we believe that focusing on the fact that this nonconstructive distance and alignment setting still has such simple algorithms that are provably correct and efficient are the main takeaway of the paper. Hence, the significance of the implementation should not be overestimated.

8. Section 6 now makes clear that the given approach only applies to a structurally restricted class of models, and calls for work that will incorporate parallelism, branching, synchronisation, resources, and data, in addition to a wider variety of distances.

9. Hyphenation should look smoother now throughout the paper.

10. The arXiv version mentioned by reviewer 1 is a preprint, submitted to allow the submission to be accessible before formal publication, a standard practice. We do not believe that the existence of a preprint should be an argument against acceptance.

> Reviewer #1: This submission proposes an approach to align series of 
> timestamps for which certain constraints hold. These constraints are all 
> of the form that the next timestamp in the event should be in some 
> predefined interval. The authors apply this approach to the situation 
> where models and event logs need to be aligned (called conformance 
> checking in the area of process mining). They propose first to align a 
> trace from the event log to the model, and second to align the 
> timestamps. The authors report that the approach does not work for 
> models where concurrency (parallelism) is present, as the timestamp of 
> the next event should be causally related to the timestamp of the 
> current event in their approach. For aligning the timestamps, they 
> propose two alignment moves: a stamp move, which changes only the 
> timestamp of the current event, and a delay move, which changes the 
> timestamp of the current and all following events. A linear algorithm is 
> provided to solve this alignment problem.
